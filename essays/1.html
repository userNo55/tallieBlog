Tired of YouTube? I know why. Me too.

Is it possible to be around people for years and remain completely unnoticed, uninteresting, and a stranger to them?


This question comes to my mind when I see channels with thousands of videos but only a couple of dozen views on them. The author has been doing this for several years. They record, do at least some minimal editing, and post it. People say, "They are hardly expecting big results; they are probably doing it for themselves, they just like it, a small cozy community can be more comfortable."

To me, these are just excuses. I could never not expect the best every single time I hit the Publish button. I think people are scared of such an amount of failure. It scares them that hard work, persistence, and a positive attitude—none of this guarantees you anything good. This is an uncomfortable truth.

I have been living like this for many years. No matter what I try, I fail, and it doesn't matter how much effort I put in or how much money I spend. I think these are exactly the kind of people pseudo-witches lure in with stories about curses. But of course, it's not mysticism—it's statistics. There is always a chance of failure, and there is no reason not to hit a total losing streak.

And algorithms work exactly the same way: just statistics, math, and algebra. Of course, it is sad to realize that there are very few people in the world who enjoy what you create. But those channels—those quiet channels—keep releasing videos. Consistently.

They talk to the camera about their impressions of movies, for example. And someone is subscribed and watches these videos every week. If they stay, they probably write comments and they probably write comments and talk to the author. How does it happen that this is not enough for growth? There are so many weird and unstable characters on the internet, yet a normal, real person who is persistent and hardworking turns out to be somehow not enough. YouTube is a huge platform full of people from all over the world, but even here, only a couple of random passersby on a bench listen to you while they wait for the bus.

Meanwhile, others can get monetized from their very first 8-minute video. Maybe, of course, that video is objectively better, but the influence of luck cannot be denied either. It would seem that YouTube is very democratic, but time and again I find channels whose success would be impossible without a standard of living unavailable to most. Even if these people call themselves the "middle class."

Well, have you lost your train of thought yet? 99.99% have already turned off this video long ago. It doesn't turned off this video long ago. It doesn't stick to a clear Promise—give what you promised, argue logically, but not too complex, not too multi-layered. Is it hard to maintain focus in my video? Yes? Even more so in today's world. I'm not saying you will learn something, feel smarter or more informed, or get validation for your radical biases. This is just a stranger's stream of consciousness about something familiar.

"She clearly lacks friends!"

Of course I do! I'm glad it's different for you, but statistics show that I am not alone in my loneliness. And so, by rejecting the rules of objective audience retention and attraction, I am manually throwing myself into an algorithmic desert.

Why? Is this self-sabotage? Will I meet anyone at all out here?

I don't know.

And that's why I keep doing it.
